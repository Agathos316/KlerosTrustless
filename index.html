How is Kleros Trustless?

In technological terms, 'trustless' refers to a system where there is no necessity to trust in a third-party or intermediary. Users instead trust the system, which is autonomous, transparent, and openly verifiable. Such a system is inherently trustworthy and predictable by nature of its core design. This page explains the core elements that help make Kleros a trustless system.

Kleros implements the four rules of 'credible neutrality' as follows:

  1. No bias or favouritism: Specific people or outcomes are not embedded into Kleros. No one has inherently more control than anyone else. Jurors are selected randomly and are not known to each other. The Kleros architecture is also unable to influence or prefer certain outcomes.
  2. Open-source and verifiable: The system is open-source, meaning that anyone can look at the code and see what it is doing. The system's execution is also publicly verifiable, being recorded on independent databases (e.g. etherscan) accessible by everyone.
  3. Kept simple: The system is kept simple through having a variety of Kleros courts, each one reserved for a specific category of disputes. This keeps each court simple and focussed. Jurors, likewise, only operate in courts matching their expertise, preventing juror fatigue. Governance of Kleros is also kept separate to the courts, ensuring each is kept simple and focused.
  4. Does not change often: The system cannot change except by governance votes instigating a change. This is rare. Changelessness is also a feature of individual disputes, as rules governing a dispute are immutable once the dispute is initiatied.

The governance of Kleros is trustless because:

  • The Governor is a smart contract fully under the jurisdiction of votes of all PNK holders, in proportion to their holdings.
  • Voting is fee-less, enabling equitable participation amongst all PNK holders.
  • Changes to the Kleros policies, court structures, function parameters, or the underlying smart contracts are determined via a decentralized voting mechanism (Snapshot). Changes that are voted in are implemented immutably by the decentralized Governor smart contract. The smart contracts are also always open-source.
  • The full suite of Kleros verification and dispute resolution tools are available during this process.

Juror dynamics and the use of PNK help to make Kleros trustless because

  • Jurors select sub-courts matching their expertise. From there, jurors are randomly selected to serve in disputes as they arise. The probability of a juror being selected is proportional to how much PNK they have staked in Kleros.
  • Jurors have no knowledge of, or interaction with, other jurors. There is no opportunity for collusion or influencing other jurors.
  • Jurors voting with the majority are rewarded in PNK, while incoherent voting is penalized with a PNK loss. Jurors are therefore inherently incentivized to only vote honestly and responsibly.
  • A successful 51% attack is extremely difficult and costly to achieve. However, if the Kleros community believe the platform has been compromised (such as by a 51% attack), the entire system can be 'cloned' with a new version of PNK. This creates an identical system but without the attacker's PNK, thus removing their influence. This process is known as 'forking' in blockchain terminology. Forking is a failsafe mechanism in case of such attacks.
